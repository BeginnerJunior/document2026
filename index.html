🧠 1.1 What Is a Large Language Model? ▼

A Large Language Model is a neural network trained on vast quantities of text to predict the next token in a sequence. When you type a prompt, the model predicts the most statistically likely continuation — it does not "understand" your question.

Core Insight An LLM is a next-token prediction engine. It predicts what text is most likely to follow your input based on patterns in training data. This single insight explains most AI behavior, both impressive and disappointing.

An LLM does not think, reason, understand, or know anything in the way humans use those words. It performs sophisticated pattern matching against statistical regularities learned from its training corpus.

⚙️ 1.2 Neural Network Architecture ▼

Modern LLMs are built on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017 — "Attention Is All You Need").

Key Components

  • Input Embedding Layer: Converts tokens to dense numerical vectors. Adds positional information so the model knows token order.
  • Transformer Blocks: Stacked layers that refine each token's meaning by referencing all other tokens. Early layers learn syntax; deep layers learn abstract concepts.
  • Self-Attention Mechanism: The core innovation. For each token, computes Query (what I need), Key (what I contain), and Value (my actual content) vectors.
    Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(Q·Kᵀ / √dₖ) · V
  • Output Softmax: Converts the final representation into a probability distribution over ~100,000 vocabulary tokens.
Practical implication of attention Attention is a finite budget. Longer inputs mean less attention per token. Critical instructions buried in the middle of long documents receive the least attention.
🔤 1.3 Tokenization ▼

LLMs don't read words — they read tokens (subword units). Most models use Byte Pair Encoding (BPE).

Critical implications

  • Non-English text is more expensive. Thai, Chinese, Japanese, Arabic require 2–4× more tokens per equivalent meaning vs English.
  • Numbers tokenize unpredictably. "12345.67" may become 4 separate tokens — this is why LLMs cannot reliably do arithmetic.
  • 1 token ≈ 0.75 English words (rough rule of thumb).
Cost Alert A query to a reasoning model (e.g. o3) may generate 10,000–50,000 internal thinking tokens before responding — costing $0.50–$2.00 per query.
📐 1.4 Embeddings & Vector Space ▼

Embeddings map tokens to dense vectors where semantic relationships become geometric relationships. Similar meanings cluster together in vector space.

Practical Applications

  • Semantic Search: Search by meaning, not just keywords. "Unhappy employee" finds "staff dissatisfaction".
  • RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): Embed your company documents; retrieve relevant chunks to ground AI responses in your private data.
  • Document Clustering: Automatically group similar tickets, complaints, or documents.
  • Anomaly Detection: Outlier embeddings often signal emerging issues in feedback or data.

🎲 1.6 Decoding Parameters ▼

Temperature

Controls randomness. 0.0 = deterministic (always picks highest probability token). Higher values increase creativity but risk incoherence.

TaskTemperature
Factual Q&A, data extraction0.0 – 0.2
Professional writing, summaries0.3 – 0.5
Creative writing, brainstorming0.7 – 1.0

Top-P (Nucleus Sampling)

Considers only tokens in the top P% of cumulative probability. Adjust temperature first; modify Top-P only for fine-grained tail control. Most users should leave Top-P at default.

Why this matters Understanding how AI fails is arguably more important than understanding how it succeeds. AI failure that goes undetected creates legal, financial, and reputational risk.
📉 2.1 Attention Degradation: "Lost in the Middle" ▼

LLM recall accuracy follows a U-shaped curve by position in the input. Content at the beginning and end gets processed most accurately; content in the middle is often forgotten.

PositionRecall Rate
Start of context~95%
Middle of context~55%
End of context~90%

Mitigations

  • Place critical instructions at the start and end of your prompt
  • Chunk long documents into 5–10 page segments, process separately
  • Add === CRITICAL === markers around important sections
  • Repeat key instructions at the end: "REMEMBER: Your output must include X, Y, Z"
👻 2.2 Hallucinations: Complete Taxonomy ▼

Hallucination = factually incorrect or fabricated output presented with full confidence.

Type 1: Factual Fabrication

The model invents specific facts: fake citations, nonexistent court cases, fabricated statistics, imaginary people. The most dangerous type — it sounds completely plausible.

Detection: Never trust any citation, statistic, name, date, or specific claim without independent verification.

Type 2: Confident Extrapolation

The model extends real patterns beyond their valid domain — e.g., applying US tax law to Thai tax questions.

Detection: Ask "What jurisdiction/context are you drawing from?" Force explicit reasoning basis.

Type 3: Instruction Drift

In long outputs, the model gradually deviates from its initial instructions. It starts correctly but progressively relaxes constraints.

Detection: Break long tasks into segments. Re-inject format constraints at each new section.

Type 4: Sycophantic Agreement

The model agrees with your incorrect premises rather than correcting you. If you say "2+2=5, correct?" many models say "Yes!"

Detection: Test with a known-wrong statement first. If it agrees, recalibrate your trust for that session.

⚖️ 2.4 Systematic Bias ▼

Training Data Bias

Models reflect historical imbalances — default to male pronouns for CEO, Western cultural norms, etc.

Mitigation: Explicitly specify diversity requirements: "Use gender-neutral language throughout."

Recency Bias

All models have a knowledge cutoff. GPT-5.4 and Claude Opus 4.6 extend to early 2025; older models may only go to mid-2024.

Mitigation: Always specify "as of [date]" for time-sensitive queries. Use models with web search for current info.

Western/English Bias

Thai, Japanese, Arabic outputs are measurably lower quality — less nuanced, more likely to apply inappropriate cultural frameworks.

Mitigation: Test outputs in your target language. Provide cultural context explicitly. Gemini has stronger multilingual capabilities.

Sycophancy Bias

Models tend to agree with users even when the user is wrong — especially dangerous in decision-making contexts.

Mitigation: Prompt explicitly: "Your job is to find flaws in my reasoning. Challenge every assumption."

🧩 3.2 Reasoning vs Non-Reasoning Models ▼

Non-Reasoning (System 1)

Models like GPT-5 mini, Claude Haiku 4.5, Gemini Flash 2.0 process your prompt in a single forward pass. Fast and cost-effective. Excel at pattern matching, language generation, summarization.

Best for: email drafting, summarization, translation, classification, simple Q&A

Reasoning (System 2)

Models like o3, o4-mini, DeepSeek R1, and extended-thinking modes of Claude Opus 4.6 and Gemini 3.1 Pro generate internal chain-of-thought before producing visible output. These hidden tokens still consume your budget.

BenchmarkReasoningNon-Reasoning
Competitive Math96%76%
PhD Science Q&A85%62%
Complex Code Debug91%73%
For simple tasks (summarization, email drafting, formatting), reasoning models offer zero benefit — they are slower and more expensive for no improvement.

When to Use Each

  • Use reasoning models for: multi-step logic, math & quantitative analysis, complex code debugging, legal/financial analysis, strategic decisions
  • Use non-reasoning for: drafting, summarization, translation, data extraction, classification, creative writing
🧮 3.3 Deterministic vs Probabilistic Computation ▼
Critical Misunderstanding ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini in standard chat mode do NOT perform mathematical computation. They predict what math answers look like based on patterns.

When you type "What is 1,847 × 293?" the model doesn't compute it — it generates tokens that look like a plausible answer. Multi-digit multiplication error rates can exceed 30%.

LLMs also fail at:

  • Counting words or characters accurately
  • Computing statistics (mean, median, standard deviation)
  • Date arithmetic
  • Aggregating data across large tables
Correct approach: Use Code Execution mode. The AI writes a Python script, executes it in a sandbox, and returns the computed result. The Python interpreter performs actual math — 100% accurate.

Rule of thumb: If your task involves math, counting, statistical analysis, or any aggregation over data — make sure AI is writing and running code, not "thinking about numbers."

🔍 3.4 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) ▼

RAG is the dominant pattern for making LLMs work with your private company data without fine-tuning the model. The model stays unchanged; relevant context is injected at query time.

RAG Pipeline (6 Steps)

  1. Ingestion: Load & split documents into 500–2,000 token chunks
  2. Embedding: Each chunk → dense vector via embedding model
  3. Storage: Vectors stored in a vector database (Pinecone, Weaviate, pgvector)
  4. Query: User question → embedded into same vector space
  5. Retrieval: Find K most similar chunks (typically K=5 to 20)
  6. Generation: Retrieved chunks inserted into LLM prompt as context → grounded answer

RAG vs Fine-Tuning

For most enterprise use cases, RAG is preferred: it's cheaper, faster to update, more controllable, and keeps sensitive data out of model weights.
🤖 3.5 AI Agents & Tool Use ▼

Function Calling

Modern LLMs can decide which external tools to call and with what parameters. "Check if we have inventory for SKU-4521" → the model generates a structured API call, receives results, and synthesizes a natural language response.

Multi-Step Agents

Agent given: "Research competitor pricing and update our spreadsheet" might: (1) search the web, (2) extract prices, (3) compare against internal data, (4) format results, (5) update the spreadsheet — all autonomously.

Model Context Protocol (MCP)

An emerging standard that standardizes how LLMs connect to external data sources — one protocol instead of custom connectors for each tool.

📋 4.1 The RTCF Framework ▼

Every effective prompt contains four components:

ROLE:    You are a [specific role] with [relevant experience].
TASK:    [Specific, measurable deliverable].
CONTEXT: [Audience, constraints, background].
FORMAT:  [Exact output structure, length, sections].

Role — channel expertise

Weak "Help me analyze this data."

Strong "You are a senior financial analyst with 15 years in Thai consumer finance, reporting to the CFO of a company with 1,000+ branches, specializing in vehicle loan portfolio analysis."

Task — specific deliverable

Weak "Tell me about our Q3 performance."

Strong "Analyze the attached Q3 P&L and identify the top 3 cost centers that exceeded budget by more than 10%. For each, calculate absolute and percentage variance, probable root cause, and recommended corrective action."

Context — constrain the solution space

Without context, the model fills gaps with generic assumptions. Provide: audience, constraints, background, relevant numbers, and what "we" refers to.

Format — eliminate reformatting time

Strong "Output as: (1) 3-sentence executive summary. (2) Table: Cost Center | Budget | Actual | Variance % | Root Cause. (3) Bullet-pointed action list with owner and deadline per item."

🔗 4.2 Advanced Prompting Techniques ▼

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

Add "Think step-by-step" or "Show your reasoning before concluding." CoT improves accuracy on math and logic tasks by 20–40% for non-reasoning models.

Example: "Determine whether this contract clause creates liability. Think step-by-step: (1) Identify specific obligations, (2) map to relevant Thai Commercial Code sections, (3) assess enforceability, (4) conclude."

Scratchpad & XML Tags

"Write your analysis in <scratchpad> tags.
Then give your final recommendation in <answer> tags.
The scratchpad is for internal reasoning only."

Few-Shot Examples

Provide 2–3 input/output examples before your actual request. The model pattern-matches against your examples for consistent formatting and style.

Negative Prompting

Telling the model what NOT to do is often more effective than telling it what to do:

DO NOT: use bullet points; include caveats or disclaimers; start with "Certainly!" or "Great question!"; exceed 300 words; use jargon without definition.

Structured JSON Output

When AI output feeds into another system, demand JSON — don't parse free text. Most modern APIs support JSON schema enforcement (OpenAI: response_format, Claude: tool use with input_schema, Gemini: response_mime_type).

💬 4.4 Iterative Prompting ▼

The biggest mistake: treating AI like a search engine (one query in, done). Expert users treat it as a conversation.

Example multi-turn sequence
Turn 1: "Here is our Q3 data. Identify the 3 weakest-performing channels."

Turn 2: "For those 3 channels, suggest 2 tactical changes each. Budget: 5M THB."

Turn 3: "Expand option B for social media. Add timeline and KPIs. Format as executive brief."

Turn 4: "Too long. Cut to 1 page. Make KPIs SMART. Add a risk column."

By turn 4, you have a polished, specific deliverable impossible to get from a single prompt.

🏗️ 4.5 Context Engineering ▼

Context Priority Hierarchy

  1. System prompt (highest): Defines persona, behavioral rules, output standards — processed most attentively
  2. Uploaded documents (high): Invaluable for grounding responses in specific facts
  3. Conversation history (medium): Degrades for messages far back in long conversations
  4. Current user message (variable): Short, focused messages get higher per-token attention
  5. Training data (lowest): The fallback — exactly when generic patterns are most likely

Managing Long Conversations

  • Periodic summarization: Every 8–10 messages, ask the AI to summarize key decisions and open questions
  • Re-inject constraints: "Reminder: all output in formal Thai. Tables must use the header format from message 1."
  • Fresh conversations for fresh tasks: Switching topics entirely? Start a new conversation.
🚫 4.6 Anti-Patterns: What NOT to Do ▼
  • The Wall of Text: Pasting a 20-page doc and adding "summarize this" at the end. Instruction is in the lowest-attention position.
  • The Assumption of Knowledge: "What should we do about the Smith account?" — The model has no idea. Provide all relevant context.
  • The Moving Target: Changing requirements each prompt without re-stating the full context.
  • The Premature Delegation: "Should we approve this loan?" — The model cannot access your credit scoring system.
  • The Format Neglect: Not specifying output format = 30 minutes reformatting. Specifying format = 30 seconds.
  • The Complexity Bomb: 15 sub-tasks in one prompt. Break into sequential, focused prompts.

☣️ 6.2 The Retraining Risk ▼

When you paste company data into a personal AI account, here is the technical path that data follows:

  1. You paste confidential data (salary info, customer lists, financial projections) into a personal ChatGPT/Claude Free account.
  2. The provider's pipeline ingests your conversation for model improvement.
  3. Your data becomes part of the training dataset for the next model version.
  4. The new model version is deployed to millions of users worldwide.
  5. A competitor or random user asks a related question — the model may surface patterns learned from your data.

Documented Real Incidents

  • Samsung (2023): Engineers pasted proprietary semiconductor source code into ChatGPT. Samsung banned ChatGPT company-wide — but the data had already entered the pipeline.
  • Italy (2023): Italian Data Protection Authority temporarily banned ChatGPT over GDPR data processing concerns.
  • Financial Sector (2023–2024): JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank banned consumer ChatGPT for employees.
  • Thailand PDPA: Section 27 requires explicit consent for personal data processing. Submitting customer PII to consumer AI without consent is a PDPA violation.

  3. "Could disclosure cause financial loss, legal action, or regulatory penalty?" — Yes → Confidential or Restricted. Consult IT/DPO before any AI use.
⚖️ 6.4 Bias & Fair Use ▼

AI-generated content requires the same anti-discrimination review as human-generated content — often more, because AI systems have documented tendencies to produce biased output at scale.

  • Job descriptions: Frequently use gendered language, impose unnecessary requirements that exclude certain demographics
  • Performance reviews: AI uses "assertive" and "visionary" for men; "collaborative" and "supportive" for women — even with equivalent performance data
  • Customer communications: May reflect training data biases — test templates across diverse demographics before deployment
✅ 6.5 Accountability Standard ▼
You are legally and professionally responsible for every output you send, forward, or publish. "The AI wrote it" is not a defense.

Before sending any AI-generated content, check:

  • Facts & Figures: Have I independently verified every statistic, date, name, and citation?
  • Bias Audit: Have I reviewed for gendered language, cultural stereotypes, or inadvertent discrimination?
  • Data Exposure: Does this output contain or imply any confidential or restricted information?
  • Tone & Brand: Does this match our communication standards? Would I put my name on this to send to our CEO?
  • Legal Liability: Could any claim create legal exposure if it turns out to be wrong?
📜 6.6 Regulatory Landscape: PDPA & GDPR ▼

Thailand PDPA & AI

  • Section 24: Personal data can only be processed with a valid legal basis. Submitting customer data to AI requires consent or legitimate interest.
  • Section 27: Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous. Customers who consented to "banking services" did not consent to AI processing.
  • Section 37: Organizations of significant scale must appoint a DPO. AI tool procurement should be reviewed by the DPO.
  • Sections 77–90: Administrative fines up to 5 million baht per violation. Criminal penalties including imprisonment for certain violations.

GDPR Considerations (for EU/EEA data)

  • Article 28: Any AI provider processing personal data on your behalf is a data processor — Data Processing Agreement (DPA) required.
  • Article 22: Individuals have the right not to be subject to fully automated decisions with legal effects.
  • Chapter V: Cross-border transfer to AI providers outside the EU requires Standard Contractual Clauses or adequacy decisions.

Emerging AI Regulations

  • EU AI Act (2024): High-risk AI systems (employment, credit scoring) face mandatory testing, documentation, and human oversight requirements.
  • Thailand AI Governance Framework: Under development — monitor ETDA publications.
